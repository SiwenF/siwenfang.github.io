From Ambiguity to Confidence: Optimizing the E2E Withdrawal Journey

A forensic gap analysis of the legacy flow revealed that drop-offs weren’t just due to UI friction, but a fundamental breakdown in trust. Users faced “black-box” processing, ambiguous network selections leading to high churn and a surge in “Where are my funds?” support tickets.

Role
Lead Designer & Researcher

Key issues

  • Unclear rules (limits, fees, processing time)

  • Poor status

From Benchmark to Build

Withdrawals are the most high-anxiety touchpoint in the crypto ecosystem. For a global exchange, this journey was plagued by high drop-off rates due to “Information Overload,” confusing network fee structures, and a lack of transparency regarding blockchain confirmation times. Users felt a sense of “loss of control” once they hit the submit button, leading to a high volume of urgent support tickets and brand distrust.

1

Journey Mapping & Competitive Benchmarking

I documented every step from intent to confirmation on web and app, then recreated parallel journeys for Kraken and Coinbase. The output was a single view: same task, three products—so stakeholders could see where OKX added friction, where copy was ambiguous, and where global users (different locales, networks, and compliance expectations) would struggle.

2

Gap Analysis & Pixel-Level Deconstruction

The benchmark exposed a structural gap: Kraken and Coinbase split the journey into focused steps (select token, select network, input amount, input address), while OKX condensed almost everything into one dense page. I audited hierarchy, spacing, component states, and copy at pixel level to pinpoint where overload happened. In the legacy layout, token/network controls, warnings, fees, and secondary details competed in the same visual field, spacing was tight, and redundant information reduced readability at the exact moment users needed confidence.

Gap vs competitors

Step structure vs. single-screen overload

Kraken and Coinbase progressively disclosed decisions across steps. OKX packed selection, validation, and confirmation cues into one screen, increasing scan burden and hesitation.

Gap vs competitors

Token/network controls were hard to parse

Critical choices sat too close together with limited spacing and weak grouping, so users struggled to distinguish token selection from network selection and associated risks.

Global scale

Redundant and competing information

Repeated helper text and mixed-priority content diluted key actions. Important data (fees, limits, and timing) did not stand out clearly from secondary details.

Trust & status

Low readability under high-stakes decisions

Under stress, dense copy and tight vertical rhythm made the page feel unclear. The issue was not one missing element, but cumulative friction from layout, hierarchy, and copy.

“The competitive walkthrough made it obvious: we weren’t failing on one screen—we were failing on the cumulative weight of small decisions.”

— Workshop synthesis (internal)
3

Redesign for Global, Scalable Users

I redesigned the flow with progressive disclosure and clearer hierarchy: consistent components across locales, translation-ready copy, and resilient states for edge cases. Security stayed strict, but the experience feels protective, not punitive, with clearer confirmations and status visibility throughout.
